How to Make Lavender Essential Oils

Melbren

Essential oils are known to provide therapeutic and calming benefits, especially when used for aromatherapy. And when we talk about 'calming,' one scent always stands out - Lavender. In this article, we'll talk about how to make Lavender essential oils in a few simple steps. So gather up those Lavender flowers, and let's jump in.

How to make lavender essential oils at home?

Fragrance oils can get a bit pricey. But one of the most popular, Lavender oil, is easy enough to make on your own. You'd only need a few items, and making them at home could be much cheaper, plus you can make lots of it in one batch.

However, keep in mind that it could not be as potent as the ones you buy from stores. But hey, it's all-natural, and it could still work as well as the commercial variants.

Stuff you'll need:

To make Lavender essential oils, you must prepare the following:

  • An airtight glass container
  • Dried Lavender flowers
  • Your choice of carrier oil (more info on this below)
  • Cheesecloth or a fine-mesh strainer
  • Funnel
  • A jar or bottle to store your Lavender oil
  • Mortar and pestle

Procedure:

Step 1

If you have a bunch of dried Lavenders attached to its stalk, pick out the flowers, and lightly crush them using the mortar and pestle. This will help release the scent of the flowers. We'll only be using the flowers since it's the primary source of the scent. You can also include some of the leaves, but it should mostly be the flowers.

Step 2

Once you've picked out all your Lavender flowers, put them inside your airtight glass container. The size of the container and how many flowers you'll put inside is mainly up to you. But we suggest you fill in your container at least 3/4 full with Lavender flowers.

Step 3

Pour in your carrier oil and fill the airtight glass container. Make sure your Lavender flowers are entirely soaked and submerged with oil. You can mix two types of oils if you want to, such as olive oil and almond oil.

Step 4

Let your Lavender oil mixture sit for 1-3 weeks. It's best if you put your jar somewhere warm or sunny (but not direct sunlight) to help it infuse a lot better. Don't forget to turn and shake your container occasionally as the flowers may float on oil.

Step 5

After 3 weeks, strain your Lavender oil mixture to remove the flowers. You can use a fine-mesh strainer by placing it on a bowl and pouring your Lavender oil mixture all through. But if you want to juice out all the good 'ol bits from those Lavender flowers, we recommend using cheesecloth instead of (or with) a strainer. You can then wring the cloth and squeeze all the remaining oil from the Lavender flowers.

Step 6

Transfer your freshly-made Lavender oil into the jar or bottle where you'd want to store it. This will most likely have a wonderful mild scent that is not too overpowering when you use it. If you wish to make the smell more intense, you can mix it with store-bought Lavender essential oil since these are a lot more potent than homemade ones. Just be careful when adding more potent essential oils since yours is pretty much scented already.

Fun fact: Commercial or store-bought Lavender essential oils undergo a more complicated process like steam distillation. It also has a smaller yield than a homemade variant, which explains why they could be a bit pricey.

Shelf-life

Homemade Lavender oils can last up to 1 year. Make sure to keep it in a cool, dry, and not too sunny place to prolong its shelf-life.

How do you make Lavender oil from fresh Lavender?

If you happen to harvest or simply get your hands on a bunch of fresh Lavender, you can turn them into Lavender oil as well.

You can follow the same procedure we mentioned above. However, you must first dry your fresh Lavender before proceeding with the steps above. Using fresh Lavender directly is not advisable because it could spoil your Lavender oil. Your flowers should be completely dry to prolong the shelf life of your essential oil.

Pro tip: If you're the type who keeps Lavender flowers in a vase in your home, don't throw them away when they are wilted and dry. You can use your dried up Lavender to make Lavender oil! 

How to dry your Lavender?

Step 1

Cut your fresh Lavender into stalks (about 6 inches long).

Step 2

Group them into small bunches and tie the stalks together. Think of it as making a small Lavender bouquet.

Step 3

Hang your 'Lavender bouquets' upside down in a warm and dry place. It's best if you place them somewhere with occasional sunlight to help them dry better.

Step 4

Let your Lavender flowers hang like this for at least 2-3 weeks to make sure they're completely dry. After 2-3 weeks, you can make your Lavender oil mixture (follow the procedure listed above). 

How much Lavender does it take to make essential oil?

The amount of Lavender you'd need for homemade Lavender mostly depends on how much Lavender oil you want to make. You must first choose the size of the container where you'll infuse your Lavender oil. The best ratio is to fill 3/4 of your container with Lavender flowers. Then fill the rest of the container with a carrier oil.

Types of carrier oil

Carrier oils come in different types. Most of them don't evaporate quickly compared to commercial essential oils and have various medicinal properties. You can choose from different varieties of carrier oils, depending on your needs.

Here is a list of the different carrier oils you can use to make your Lavender essential oil. These oils are listed according to their comedogenic scale (since you'll most likely use your Lavender oil for different purposes).

This Comedogenic scale ranking is based on how likely the oil may clog your pores. 0 being the non-clogging and 5 being the highest possibility of it to clog your pores.

What is the best Lavender for an essential oil?

Lavender, or Lavandula, is a flowering plant that belongs to the mint family. There are about 47 known Lavender species, but some of them are specially grown and cultivated for commercial purposes or as ornamental plants for landscape use.

Among these 47 species of Lavender plants, 2 stands out the most when making essential oils. These two are the following:

1. Lavandula angustifolia or English Lavender

The English Lavender is one of the most popular Lavender species and is widely used in making essential oils. They usually have dark purple flowers with contrasting blue-green foliage. Even when dried, the English Lavender retains its color.

This flower yields an oil with sweet overtones, making them perfect for cosmetics, perfumes, salves, essential oils, and more.

2. Lavandula × intermedia or Lavandin

This Lavender species is a crossbreed between English Lavender and Portuguese Lavender. They grow larger and have more flower spikes compared to the English Lavender.

Lavandin also has a more medicinal aroma because of its higher camphor content, which produces a sharper overtone to its fragrance. 

Benefits of Lavender oil

Lavender essential oil is definitely a cult-favorite. Mainly because it doesn't only smell good, it's also versatile. Apart from aromatherapy, Lavender essential oils have tons of other uses. It also has many beauty and health benefits, which you might not know.

Relieves headache and nausea

Lavender oils come in handy when you suffer from headaches. Studies show that applying Lavender oil to your temples, forehead, and behind your ears could relieve headaches. Inhaling the scent of Lavender oils can also help alleviate motion sickness and nausea.

Minimize acne

Lavender is known to have antiseptic properties. Its antibacterial components can help treat and prevent acne, thus keeping your skin heal faster.

Nourishes skin

You can use Lavender oil to nourish your skin. It's packed with antioxidants to keep your skin looking younger.

Soothes the nervous system

According to studies, inhaling Lavender's scent has a calming and soothing effect and helps calm the nervous system. This can also help relieve stress, anxiety, and fatigue.

Heal minor wounds

Applying Lavender oil mixed with some water can help stimulate cell regeneration and heal some minor wounds faster.
